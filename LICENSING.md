# Licensing

MAP uses two licenses so it's easy to build on, while authorship stays credited.

## TL;DR

| What | License | You can | You must |
|------|---------|---------|----------|
| **Code** — `cli/`, `reference/`, `examples/`, scripts | [MIT](LICENSE) | Use, copy, modify, fork, ship commercially | Keep the copyright notice |
| **Content** — `patterns/`, `docs/`, prose, diagrams | [CC BY 4.0](LICENSE-CONTENT) | Use, share, adapt, even commercially | **Give attribution** to MAP / the author |
| **Name & brand** — "MAP", "Missing AI Patterns", logos | Reserved (not licensed) | — | Use a different name for forks |

In short: **take the ideas and code and run with them — just credit MAP for the
writing, and don't ship your fork under the MAP name.**

## Why two licenses

- **Code as MIT** keeps the tooling maximally reusable — anyone can build analyzers,
  integrations, or products on top without friction.
- **Content as CC BY 4.0** is the norm for knowledge bases. The catalog can be quoted,
  translated, and adapted freely, but the work must be **attributed**, so contributors
  keep credit for what they wrote.
- **Reserving the name/brand** prevents someone from re-publishing MAP as if it were
  their own project. Forks are welcome — under their own name.

## How to attribute content

When you reuse or adapt a pattern or doc, add a credit like:

> Based on MAP — Missing AI Patterns (https://github.com/rajanbor/map), licensed under
> CC BY 4.0. Changes were made.

(Only mention changes if you made them.)

## Contributions

By contributing you agree that your contributions are licensed to the project and its
users under these terms: **code under MIT, content under CC BY 4.0** (inbound =
outbound). You keep the copyright to your contributions.
